# Ontology (secondary feature)

> The rich company ontology is **kept**, not dropped. It is deliberately sequenced behind the compliance wedge.

## What it is

The original vision — a full engineering knowledge graph of **teams, projects, services, repositories, libraries, releases, owners, and contact persons**, and how they all relate. It is the foundation model Bojji owns (confirmed spec **C1**).

## Primary vs. secondary

| Role | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Primary — what we sell** | Compliance-grade **exposure & blast-radius**. The mandatory, paid reason to adopt. |
| **Secondary — kept, rides along** | The org ontology: teams, projects, owners, **contact persons**, and project/service relationships. |

## Why it's secondary, not primary

- An org catalog **doesn't sell on its own** — that market is commoditised (Backstage, Cortex, Port).
- Its ownership and contact data is the part that **needs human input**.

So it rides along with compliance rather than leading the pitch.

## Why we keep it anyway

It makes every exposure answer **actionable**. Without the ontology, exposure is just a list of repositories. With it, the answer is routed, owned, and contactable:

> "6 products across 3 teams are exposed to this vulnerability — here is each product, its owning team, and who to notify."

It also preserves the path to the broader knowledge-graph vision.

## Two horizons — don't confuse them

- **Secondary (near-term):** owners, teams, contact persons — ships alongside compliance because it makes the answers useful.
- **Expansion (later):** new entity types such as incidents, deployments, APIs, and cost — added only as real queries pull for them.
