The opening

Ontology is hot — but nobody can keep one alive.

The research hands us a wedge hiding in plain sight. Two facts sit right next to each other.

Tailwind

Ontology is the reliability layer for AI

Gartner calls semantic budget "non-negotiable" and puts semantic layers and GraphRAG among its 2026 top trends. Grounding an LLM in a knowledge graph cuts hallucination hard. Everyone is racing to own it.

The gap

But building one is the unsolved problem

Construction is "largely manual, iterative, resource-intensive." LLMs that auto-generate ontologies are weak and assistive-only. And maintenance is worse — "no complete system for automated ontology evolution exists." They go stale.

Bojji's core mechanism is exactly the unsolved part. It builds its graph from code — lockfiles, CODEOWNERS — buildless, no curation, re-derived on every commit. Ownership isn't stored to rot; it's resolved live. Self-constructing, self-maintaining, always fresh.

And kill everything else

The most important slide is the "no" list.

Focus is what you refuse to build. Six things go in the bin.

  • "We generate SBOMs." → GitLab and npm sbom already do, free. Consume theirs; keep our generator only as an air-gap fallback.
  • "AI knowledge graph" as the pitch. → MCP and "query your catalog with AI" are table stakes. Keep MCP as an optional, secondary surface. Never the headline.
  • The general org-catalog ambition. → Backstage, Cortex, Port own "software catalog," and it doesn't sell alone. The ontology rides along only because it makes exposure routable.
  • "What breaks if I upgrade." → That needs code-usage analysis — Moderne's heavy game, and metadata literally can't answer it. Verb discipline: exposure, never breakage.
  • Stored ownership. → A stored owner is a future wrong answer. Derive live, stamp freshness, point at a team — never a person.
  • The word "compliance." → Until the signed attestation is proven air-gapped. Until then it's "inventory and exposure." Honest, still valuable.
